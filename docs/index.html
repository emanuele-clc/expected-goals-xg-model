Key Terms, Explained Simply

No stats or ML background needed. Click any term below to expand a plain-language explanation before diving into the charts.

What is "xG" (Expected Goals)?

A single number between 0 and 1 that estimates how likely a shot was to result in a goal, based on things like distance, angle, and defensive pressure - not whether it actually went in. A tap-in from one yard might be 0.90 xG; a strike from 30 yards might be 0.03 xG. It's a way of measuring chance quality, not just counting shots or goals.

What does "AUC" mean?

A score from 0.5 to 1.0 measuring how well the model tells dangerous shots apart from harmless ones. 0.5 is a coin flip, 1.0 is perfect. Real xG models in the industry typically land between 0.75 and 0.85 - football has a lot of irreducible randomness, so nothing gets close to 1.0, and nothing should.

What is "calibration"?

A check on whether the model's percentages are honest. If it says a group of shots each have a 20% chance of scoring, roughly 1 in 5 of them should actually go in. Good calibration is what makes an xG value meaningful, rather than just a ranking of "more or less dangerous."

What is a "bootstrap" / confidence interval?

Instead of trusting a single test result, the model is re-tested 3,000 times on randomly resampled versions of the same held-out data. This produces a range (e.g. [0.785, 0.857]) instead of one fixed number, showing how much the result could plausibly vary - and lets you check whether one model is genuinely better than another, or if the gap could just be chance.

What is "permutation importance"?

A way to measure how much a model relies on a given input, by scrambling that input on purpose and seeing how much accuracy drops. If scrambling "distance" hurts the model a lot, distance matters a lot; if scrambling "first-time strike" barely changes anything, it doesn't.

What is "cross-validation"?

Instead of training and testing on one fixed split of the data, the training portion is divided into 5 chunks; the model trains on 4 of them and tests on the 5th, five times over with a different chunk held out each time. This gives a far more reliable performance estimate than trusting a single lucky (or unlucky) split.

What does "xG over/underperformance" mean?

The gap between the goals a player or team actually scored and the total xG their shots added up to. Positive means they finished chances better than the shot quality suggested (clinical finishing, or good luck); negative means the opposite (wasteful finishing, or bad luck). It's the same "goals minus xG" metric used by outlets like Opta and Understat.

Head-to-head

Model Comparison

What this shows: AUC (area under the ROC curve) on the held-out test set (n=3,486), with 95% confidence intervals from 3,000 bootstrap resamples. AUC measures how well a model ranks shots by danger: 0.5 is random guessing, 1.0 is perfect separation of goals from misses. (This comparison is computed once on the full test set and isn't affected by the competition filter above.)

What it tells you: XGBoost (AUC 0.801) is now the best of the three trained models on this bigger, more varied dataset, narrowly ahead of gradient boosting (0.801) and logistic regression (0.799) - though the paired bootstrap test shows the gap is thin: XGBoost beats logistic regression in 68.9% of resamples and gradient boosting in only 58.6%, so treat "best" here as a mild edge, not a knockout. All three sit further behind StatsBomb's own proprietary xG (0.824) than in the smaller 3-competition version of this project - every trained model loses to StatsBomb in 100% of bootstrap resamples now (previously that gap had real overlap). The likely reason: mixing club-season shots (La Liga) with international-tournament shots asks one general model to fit two somewhat different shot-quality distributions at once, which is a harder problem than fitting either alone - a genuine, honestly-reported trade-off of the bigger dataset, not a bug.

Interpretability

Feature Importance

What this shows: permutation importance - how much the test AUC drops when a single feature's values are randomly shuffled. A bigger drop means the model relies on that feature more heavily.

What it tells you: the two best models disagree, interestingly, about which single feature matters most. Logistic regression still leans on distance (plus its log-transformed version). XGBoost (charted above, since it's the best-performing model) leans overwhelmingly on shot angle instead, with raw distance a distant second. This isn't a contradiction: angle and distance are correlated in real shot locations (shots near the byline are simultaneously far away in a straight line and at a sharp angle), so a flexible tree ensemble is free to pick whichever cleanly-separating feature it finds first, while a linear model needs the specific engineered log/interaction terms to make distance alone do the work. Headers, nearby defenders, and through-ball assists round out XGBoost's secondary factors.

Scouting Radar: Reliable Finishers

What this is: the leaderboard above ranks players by raw "goals minus xG," which treats a hot streak on 10 shots the same as a proven edge over 200. This section corrects for that: each of the 303 players with at least 15 shots has their own shot-by-shot (goal - xG) values bootstrap-resampled 2,000 times, and players are ranked by the 2.5th percentile of that distribution - a conservative "skill floor" that only stays positive if the over-performance survives a pessimistic reading of the player's own sample. Only 8 of 303 players (2.6%) clear that bar.

What it tells you: every player who clears the bar plays in La Liga 2015/16 - the only competition in this dataset with enough matches per player (a full club season vs. a handful of tournament games) to make an individual finishing-skill estimate statistically meaningful. That's not a flaw in the method, it's the method doing its job: most "elite finisher" tournament storylines are two or three big moments in a small sample, indistinguishable from luck at this level of scrutiny, and this ranking says so honestly instead of pretending otherwise. This is also exactly the kind of volume-aware, conservative signal a recruitment or scouting team would want before spending a transfer budget on a "hot" attacker: it doesn't care about reputation or price tag, only whether the shot-quality data itself supports the reputation.
